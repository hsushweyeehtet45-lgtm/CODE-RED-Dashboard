Decision Architecture

Wisner Neural Network — Forward Pass

A single-hidden-layer neural network encodes 6 socioeconomic inputs through Wisner's 5 vulnerability dimensions (PAR model) to produce a single vulnerability output (0–1). All activations use sigmoid functions.

Network Architecture — 6 → 5 → 1
Live Forward Pass

→ Input Layer (6 nodes)

Six socioeconomic features normalised to [0,1]: income_level, education_level, age, health_limitation, transport_access, household_size. These represent the raw social conditions of each agent.

⬡ Wisner Layer (5 nodes)

Hidden layer applies Wisner's Pressure and Release (PAR) model: h1_economic, h2_social, h3_physical, h4_access, h5_household. Each node captures a structural vulnerability dimension via learned weights + sigmoid activation.

◎ Output Layer (1 node)

Single sigmoid output: vulnerability rate ∈ [0,1]. Computed as weighted sum of all 5 Wisner activations + bias_out=0.05. Weights: [0.25, 0.25, 0.20, 0.20, 0.10].

Research Framework

Theoretical foundations from Wisner's Pressure and Release model, ABM design rationale, and policy applications of this simulation.

⊕ What is This ABM?

This Agent-Based Model simulates earthquake vulnerability across 3 socioeconomic scenarios. Each of the 500 agents per scenario holds 6 normalised attributes (income, education, age, health limitation, transport access, household size). At each of 50 time steps, the agent forward-passes through a Wisner-theory neural network and applies small random drift (σ=0.01) to simulate behavioural dynamics.


Total dataset: 3 scenarios × 500 agents × 50 steps = 75,000 records. Final-step analysis uses 1,500 records (one per agent).

◎ Wisner's Pressure & Release Model

Wisner et al. (2004) argue that disasters result from the intersection of a natural hazard with human vulnerability — never from the hazard alone. Vulnerability is structured along five dimensions operationalised here as the hidden layer:


• h1 Economic: income & education buffer
• h2 Social: education & community ties
• h3 Physical: age & health — physical capacity
• h4 Access: transport access to resources
• h5 Household: dependency burden & household size

⬡ Weight Matrix Design

The weight matrix W (6×5) is theory-driven, not learned from observed data. Income and education carry strong negative weights on economic (−0.70) and social (−0.70) nodes — high income & education reduce vulnerability. Age and health carry positive weights on physical (0.60, 0.80) — older, less healthy agents face higher physical vulnerability. Transport has the strongest negative weight on access (−0.90). Household size has the strongest positive weight on household burden (0.80).

△ Socioeconomic Inequality & Disasters

A consistent finding in disaster research is that outcomes correlate strongly with pre-disaster socioeconomic conditions. This simulation quantifies the vulnerability gap: Low SES communities face 29.8 percentage points higher mean vulnerability than High SES communities under the same hazard exposure. This is not a feature of the hazard — it is a structural property of social inequality.


Cohen's d = 6.42 (Low vs High SES) represents an extremely large effect size, confirming the PAR model's core claim: vulnerability is manufactured socially, not determined naturally.

◈ Predictive Models & Feature Importance

Three ML models (Neural Network, Random Forest, XGBoost) were trained on the final-step dataset (1,500 agents, 80/20 split). All achieve very high R² (>0.96) because vulnerability is deterministically computed from inputs — the ML models recover the underlying ABM mechanism. XGBoost performs best (R²=0.9912, RMSE=0.0074).


Feature importance rankings consistently identify income_level and education_level as the most influential predictors (via h1_economic and h2_social), followed by transport_access (via h4_access). This mirrors the PAR model's theoretical emphasis on economic and social root causes of vulnerability.

Formal Model Definition — Forward Pass Equations ▼

Input vector: x = [x₁_income, x₂_edu, x₃_age, x₄_health, x₅_transport, x₆_household]ᵀ ∈ [0,1]⁶


Hidden layer: z_h = Wx + b_h; h = σ(z_h) where W is the 6×5 Wisner weight matrix and b_h = [0.10, 0.10, 0.05, 0.10, 0.05]


Output: z_out = w_outᵀh + b_out; vulnerability = σ(z_out) where w_out = [0.25, 0.25, 0.20, 0.20, 0.10] and b_out = 0.05


Time dynamics: At each step t>0, agent inputs receive noise ε ~ N(0, 0.01) and are clipped to [0,1]. This models slow behavioural drift while preserving the agent's core socioeconomic profile.

Scenario Parameter Distributions ▼

Scenario 1 (High SES): income ~ N(0.70, 0.15), education ~ N(0.75, 0.15), age ~ N(0.35, 0.15), health ~ N(0.20, 0.10), transport ~ N(0.80, 0.15), household ~ N(0.25, 0.10). All clipped to [0,1].


Scenario 2 (Middle SES): income ~ N(0.50, 0.15), education ~ N(0.50, 0.15), age ~ N(0.50, 0.20), health ~ N(0.45, 0.15), transport ~ N(0.50, 0.20), household ~ N(0.50, 0.15).


Scenario 3 (Low SES): income ~ N(0.20, 0.12), education ~ N(0.20, 0.12), age ~ N(0.65, 0.20), health ~ N(0.70, 0.15), transport ~ N(0.15, 0.10), household ~ N(0.75, 0.15).

Policy Implications & Real-World Applications ▼

Transport investment: Transport access has the largest single-weight impact (−0.90 on h4_access). Improving transport connectivity in low-SES neighbourhoods directly reduces vulnerability through the access dimension.


Health programme targeting: Health limitation and age have the strongest positive weights on h3_physical. Pre-earthquake health programmes for elderly and disabled residents in vulnerable communities can meaningfully reduce this dimension.


Income & education interventions: As the highest-importance ML features, income and education represent the most powerful levers for long-term vulnerability reduction — consistent with the PAR model's emphasis on addressing root causes rather than just unsafe conditions.


Resource pre-positioning: The 29.8 pp vulnerability gap quantifies the scale of inequality that emergency management agencies must compensate for when designing pre-disaster preparedness programmes for low-SES communities.
